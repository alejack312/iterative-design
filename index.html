Part 1: Introduction

For this project, our group was matched with Uno Health, a startup that aims to increase access to Medicare by increasing the ease with which people can apply for Medicare savings. Currently, this is a platform that targets a specific user base of people that are eligible for Medicare benefits, but are also technically literate enough to create an account and upload all of the necessary legal documents. However, through user research, Uno has found that there is a large population of people that they could access if they allowed someone who is more comfortable with technology to upload the necessary documents on someone else's behalf. We were tasked with designing a mobile interface to help reach this exact population. After reading a project brief, we engaged in the design process to add caregiver intervention into the member's standard flow, allowing a caregiver to create an account and, given proper consent, complete the application for someone else (who we refer to as the caree). Over the course of sketches, low fidelity wireframes, and eventually a high fidelity Figma protoype, we iterated on this design, incorporating feedback from classmates, our professors, and Elise Livingston, a designer at Uno Health, in order to create a final product that we believe is a strong solution to the problem at hand. After developing our prototype, we shared Loom walkthroughs with Elise and conducted a meeting where she gave us feedback that may inform future iterations of our design.

Part 2: Project Brief

We started by reading our project brief to identify key problems that we were looking to solve. Namely, we tackled the task through two routes. First, we aimed to add a caregiver entry point to allow caregivers to check their caree's eligibility for Medicare benefits. Second, we wanted to add a caregiver account to the member portal that would connect the caree and caregiver and allow for communication between the two of them to mutually work together to complete the application for Medicare benefits. In going through the brief, we marked up a working copy to centralize our thoughts and distilled important questions into our public copy for Elise to address. After receiving feedback and clarifying our task, we began our design process by creating sketches

Part 3: Sketches

To begin the design process, each of our group members completed two different end to end sketches of the user flow. This provided a starting point to brainstorm ideas for how we might solve the problem at hand. Specifically, this informed our decision to create a separate member portal for caregivers. We also workshopped ideas for how we can best ensure proper consent from the caree yet still offer a lot of control to the caregiver. Finally, we thought about the best ways that we can offer safety nets in the case that caregivers or carees may not have access to the documents they need or are unsure of how to get. We then united key findings from our sketches into a low fidelity wireframe.

After revising our low-fidelity wireframes, we began to create a high-fidelity prototype. Using the brand kit provided to us and the feedback from Professor Cho and our peers, we created a style guide to ensure that our design was consistent. We used Figma to create high-fidelity versions of the two 'Check Eligibility' flowsâ€”caregiver and caree. Furthermore, we created various high-fidelity versions of the member portals. These included various states the user could find the portal in, such as the caregiver portal with a caree already connected, the caregiver portal with no caree connected, and the caree portal with a caregiver connected, etc. We linked these pages together to create a clickable prototype that we could share with Elise.

Here is a link to our initial high-fidelity prototype.

Here is a Loom walkthrough of our high-fidelity prototype. To dive more in-depth, we made a few key design choices that we believe will help the user navigate the portal with ease. First, we made deliberate uses of the colors in the brand kit to create a consistent, visually appealing, yet accessible design. The colors in the eligibility flow come from the brand kit. For the portals, we added some colors to distinguish between the caregiver and caree portals, and their different states. Furthermore, we added dropdown menus responsible for changing the language and font size if the user needs it. We also added a 'Read Aloud' button that would read elements on the page aloud for users who may need it. All buttons were set to follow the same style. Prior, some buttons were more rounded than others. We decided to make all buttons the same shape to create a consistent design. In various frames of the design, we gave users the ability to skip steps if they so choose. This is especially important for users who find themselves not having the information they need on hand.

Part 8: Conclusion

Over the course of this project, we were able to transform a project brief on a real problem that Uno Health is facing into a high-fidelity clickable prototype that offers a solution to the problem. Stepping through the entire design process from end-to-end, we gained first hand experience iterating on a design and incorporating actionable feedback from our peers and startup partner. In a future design sprint, we'd like to keep building out our prototype to include animations that guide users seamlessly through the flow. Further, we'd love to test our prototype on end users to figure out where assumptions we made may have fallen short. Finally, keeping the caregiver stakeholder at the top of our minds, we'd imagine what the portal may look like after the point of application. How might caregivers continue to provide help to their carees throughout their time on Uno Health? Exploring this route would allow us to continue realizing Uno's mission of lowering the barrier of entry to Medicare benefits for all those who are eligible.
